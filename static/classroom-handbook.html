AnCiR Classroom Handbook

Rhythms, data, and scientific inquiry — four hands-on lessons for senior secondary students. A companion to A Handbook of Chronobiological Data Analysis.

Every living thing keeps time. Your body temperature, alertness, and sleepiness all rise and fall on a roughly 24-hour cycle called a circadian rhythm. That makes biological rhythms a perfect playground for learning data analysis: the patterns are real, they matter to you personally, and — because we generate the data here — you can always check whether you found the right answer.

Each lesson below runs inside AnCiR, a free, browser-based analysis tool that needs no installation. Open the matching guided tour (the ? menu → Take a tour…) and it will load the data and walk you through each step.

A teaching idea unique to these lessons

In real science you never know the "true" answer. Here, the data is simulated with a hidden truth — a real period, a real difference, a real wave. You analyse it as if it were real, then reveal the truth and see how close you got. That is the safest possible way to learn whether a method can be trusted.

Lesson 1 · Yr 11–13

Find the hidden rhythm

"There is a cycle hidden in this data. How long is it?"

The idea

A rhythm is a pattern that repeats. The time taken for one full repeat is the period (symbol τ, "tau"); how many repeats happen per unit time is the frequency. Period and frequency are reciprocals: a rhythm with a period of 24 hours has a frequency of 1/24 cycles per hour.

Real biological data is noisy — the clean cycle is buried under random wobble. The skill in this lesson is pulling the period out of the noise. AnCiR's periodogram does this: it tests many candidate periods and scores how well each one explains the data. The best-scoring period shows up as a peak.

AnCiR walkthrough

  1. Open the tour Find the hidden rhythm. It loads a 7-day signal sampled every hour.
  2. Look at the raw scatter first. Can you see a daily cycle by eye? It is hard — that is the point.
  3. Find the periodogram plot. The x-axis is candidate period (in hours); the y-axis is how well that period fits.
  4. Read the period at the tallest peak. Write it down before revealing the answer.

Try this

Increase the noise on the data and re-read the periodogram. How much noise can you add before the peak becomes impossible to trust? This is exactly the problem real scientists face with messy recordings.

What to look for

A single sharp peak near 24 h means a strong daily rhythm. A broad or wandering peak means the period is uncertain. Several peaks can mean more than one rhythm is present (you will meet that idea again with the FFT).

Lesson 2 · Yr 9–13

Am I a night owl?

"Is this person a morning type or an evening type?"

The idea

Your body runs an internal clock that keeps you roughly in step with the day. This is one example of homeostasis: the way a body keeps its internal state stable and well-timed despite a changing world. The clock sets when you are most active and most sleepy. People differ in their phase — morning types ("larks") peak early, evening types ("owls") peak late.

An actogram is the classic way to see this. It stacks each day's activity in a row, one day under the next, so a daily pattern lines up into a vertical band. The position of that band tells you the phase.

AnCiR walkthrough

  1. Open the tour Am I a night owl?. It loads about a week of activity data.
  2. Open the actogram. Each row is one day.
  3. Find the dark/quiet band (rest) and the busy band (activity). When does activity start each day?
  4. Decide: lark or owl? Then reveal the hidden phase offset and check.

What to look for

A consistent vertical band means a stable rhythm. If the band drifts sideways day by day, the clock is not locked to 24 h — a real phenomenon called free-running.

Try this

Add a scatter of activity against hour-of-day. The same information appears as a single hump. Which display makes the timing easier to read — the actogram or the scatter? There is no single right answer; that is a real choice scientists make.

Lesson 3 · Yr 11–13

Is the difference real?

"Weekday vs weekend activity — real difference, or just chance?"

The idea

Two groups almost never have exactly equal averages, even when nothing real separates them — random variation alone creates gaps. The central question of statistics is: is this gap bigger than chance would produce?

This is statistical inference. We look at the spread within each group (the boxplot), the size of the gap between them, and how many data points we have. A big gap, tight spreads, and lots of data make a difference believable. A small gap, wide spreads, and little data do not.

AnCiR walkthrough

  1. Open the tour Is the difference real?. It loads two groups of activity values.
  2. Open the boxplot by group. Compare the medians and the spread of the boxes. Make a guess before any test.
  3. Run GroupComparison. It chooses an appropriate test and reports whether the difference is statistically significant.
  4. Reveal the hidden truth: was there really a difference built in, or was the true difference zero?

The deliberate trap

Sometimes the hidden truth is "no difference" even though the boxes look a little different. That teaches the single most important idea in data literacy: a visible gap is not automatically a real one.

Try this

Shrink the sample size and re-run the test. A difference that was "significant" with lots of data can vanish with little. More data lets you detect smaller real effects.

Lesson 4 · Yr 12–13

Sine waves are everywhere

"Can a single cosine curve describe this rhythm?"

The idea

The smoothest possible rhythm is a cosine wave. Four numbers describe it completely:

y = M + A · cos( 2π ( t − φ ) / τ )

  • M — the midline (called the mesor): the average level the wave oscillates around.
  • A — the amplitude: how far it swings above and below the midline.
  • φ — the phase: where in the cycle the peak falls (the timing).
  • τ — the period: how long one cycle takes.

Fitting this curve to data is called the cosinor method. AnCiR finds the values of M, A, and φ that best match the data, and reports R² — a number from 0 to 1 saying how much of the pattern the cosine explains. R² near 1 means the rhythm really is wave-shaped; R² near 0 means a single cosine is the wrong model.

AnCiR walkthrough

  1. Open the tour Sine waves are everywhere. It loads a rhythmic signal.
  2. Open the Cosinor node. Read off amplitude, phase, midline, period, and R².
  3. Point to where each parameter shows up on the fitted curve.
  4. Reveal the true values used to build the data and compare with your fit.

Try this

Add noise and watch R² fall. Then add a second, faster rhythm (a 12-hour component on top of the 24-hour one): a single cosine can no longer fit it well, and R² drops — a clue that the real signal is more complex than one wave.
